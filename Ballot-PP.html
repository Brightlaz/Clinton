E-Voting UX CASE STUDY

Ballot makes Nigerian Electoral process accessible through online-voting

Background

The 1999 transition of Nigeria from military to civilian (democratic) government, is a defining moment in Nigerian history, representing the beginning of the longest, uninterrupted Government since independence in 1960, 6 democratic election has been conducted ever since.


Nigeria has a population of 200million and over 84million registered voters as of March 11, 2019 ( Nigeria's last general election), and only 49.78% cast their votes.

About Ballot

Ballot is an online voting platform that enables eligible Nigerian citizens to cast vote for their respective choice canditates faster and easier by scanning their voter’s card issued by INEC to get fast access to vote. Ballot also helps citizens to lookup of results of ongoing and past election from the comfort of their homes.

Project Goal

The project goal was to build an online platform that:

  • Enables eligible citizens to cast vote seamlessly.
  • Increases the number of citizens who participate and cast their vote during election by 82%.
  • Mitigates obscurity in the electoral process.

My Role

I led the overall design direction of the project by conducting user research, usability studies, creating lo/hi-fidelity design, and prototyping

Figma, Miro, Adobe Photoshop, Illustrator, Google form.

Research

At this phase, we had limited information about the problem we were trying to solve. Everything we had imagine exists, but it is only an assumption. We needed to validate these assumptions, so we conducted a qualitative interview to gain a deep understanding of our users' frustrations, motivations and to better understand the actual problem.

I interviewed eligible citizens of Nigeria to understand how they go about with the current voting process. The research focuses on understanding their behaviors, needs, and motivations through structured observation.

Our Assumptions

People don’t go out on election day to cast votes because they feel a surge of election violence during the process.

On some occasions, there are delays in opening polling units and malfunctioning systems that interrupt voting.

Most people do not exercise their civil right to vote because they feel their vote does not count.

Pre-election violence tends to be more localized, it involves conflict between supporters of competing political parties.

Some individuals do not even have a voter’s card because the voter registration process is too tedious, frustrating, and time-consuming.

Some people find it difficult to travel back to their registered polling unit before and during the election to cast their votes.

User Interviews

Considering the variation of our users, I interviewed 3 category of people that were a reflection of our target audience, 3 each of adults, young adults, and people living with disability who can or may have exercised their civil right to vote through structured observation. The interview questions were developed to gain a deep understanding of users’ challenges and motivations.

Sample Interview Questions:

  • Could you tell me a bit about yourself?
  • What does your typical day look like?
  • What are your best 3 apps on your phone?
  • Why do you like them?
  • What do you do on election day?
  • Why do you vote?
  • What challenges did you face while voting?
  • How do you think it started?
  • What are your views on the current voting system?
  • What specific thing would you like to change about the current voting system?
  • What will you improve on the current voting system?
  • What are your views on online voting system?
  • Have you voted with an online voting system before?
  • Do you know of anyone who have used an online voting system?
  • In your own opinion what’s your take on the whole matter?
